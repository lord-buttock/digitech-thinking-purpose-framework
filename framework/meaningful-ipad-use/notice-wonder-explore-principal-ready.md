# Notice, Wonder And Explore

> Students use iPads to notice what is hard to see, hear, capture, replay, magnify, compare or return to later. They then turn those observations into better questions for investigation.

This is the first thinking-purpose category in the whole-school Meaningful iPad Use framework. It is for the start of learning: inquiry launches, provocations, object investigations, science observations, local place studies, text or image stimulus work, fieldwork, early questioning and pre-assessment.

It is not a required first step in every unit. The categories are a purpose selector. Teachers choose this category when the learning job is to slow students down, sharpen observation, make early thinking visible, gather evidence and open worthwhile questions.

The core message is:

> Not more iPad. Better iPad.

In this category, better iPad use means students are not passively looking at digital content or typing questions into boxes. They are using the iPad as an observation, evidence and curiosity tool.

## Leadership Summary

This category gives teachers a practical way to use iPads without increasing passive screen time. It aligns with Victorian Curriculum expectations that students observe, ask questions, record information, communicate ideas, use digital tools where appropriate and develop thinking strategies. It also fits the Victorian Teaching and Learning Model 2.0 because the iPad work happens after explicit modelling, in structured supported application.

The principal-facing rationale is simple:

> We are not asking teachers to use iPads more often. We are helping them know when iPads genuinely improve learning.

For Notice, Wonder And Explore, iPads are justified when they help students:

- capture real-world evidence
- zoom, crop or magnify details
- replay sound, movement or process
- use slow motion or time-lapse to notice what is too fast or slow to see live
- record oral questions before writing fluency catches up
- compare observations across groups
- revisit early questions later in a unit
- create assessable evidence of initial thinking

If the task can be done just as well with discussion, mini-whiteboards, paper, sketching, physical artefacts or sticky notes, teachers should use the non-digital method.

## Thinking Category Spec

### Thinking Job

Students observe closely, gather initial evidence, ask questions, notice patterns, explore a stimulus or phenomenon, and begin to identify what might be worth investigating.

This is the thinking of:

- looking carefully before explaining
- separating observation from interpretation
- asking better questions
- noticing change, detail, pattern, surprise or uncertainty
- making early thinking visible so it can be revisited and improved

### Best Used When

- launching a new inquiry, topic or unit
- introducing a phenomenon, object, place, text, artwork, source or problem
- students need to slow down and observe before jumping to conclusions
- students need to ask better questions, not just produce more questions
- there is real-world evidence students can capture
- something changes too quickly or too slowly to observe well live
- student oral thinking needs to be captured before writing becomes a barrier
- teachers want pre-assessment evidence of what students notice, assume and wonder

### Student Roles

- Students as observers
- Students as questioners
- Students as early researchers
- Students as collaborators
- Students as storytellers, when observations become oral, visual or multimodal accounts

### Strong iPad Artefacts

- photo evidence journals
- annotated close-up images
- audio wonderings
- short observation videos
- slow-motion clips
- time-lapse evidence
- digital field notes
- photo and question trails
- provocation galleries with cropped evidence and revised questions
- before and after observation comparisons

### Enhancement Rule

Use iPads when they help students observe, capture, replay, magnify, hear, compare, annotate, share or revisit evidence in ways that paper cannot easily support.

Use paper, talk, manipulatives, drawing or mini-whiteboards when the task is simply listing ideas, brainstorming questions, copying information, watching a video, decorating a poster or recording what students already know.

### Substitution Danger Zone

Avoid:

- digital brainstorms with no evidence
- typed question lists that could be sticky notes
- students taking decorative photos without close observation
- watching a provocation video without analysis or response
- using Freeform as a digital worksheet
- making a photo collage with no reasoning or questions
- having one confident student operate the iPad while others watch
- using iPads at the start of every lesson just because they are available

### Required Evidence Of Learning

Each activity should produce at least one assessable trace of thinking:

- a photo, audio clip or video linked to a student noticing
- an annotated image showing what was observed
- an oral or written wondering connected to evidence
- a before and after question showing improvement
- a comparison between live observation and replayed observation
- a group explanation of why a question is worth investigating
- a teacher-visible record of what students initially noticed, assumed or misunderstood

## Activity 1: Sound Detectives

**Thinking Category:** Notice, Wonder And Explore  
**Student Role:** Students as observers and questioners  
**Year Level:** F-2  
**Best Tool:** Camera, Voice Memos, Clips  
**Thinking Tool:** See, Think, Wonder adapted as `I hear`, `I notice`, `I wonder`

### Teacher Setup

Set up 3 or 4 safe sound stations:

- vibrating ruler
- rice shaker
- tapping different materials
- water in containers
- classroom object sound mystery

Model one example:

> I noticed the sound changed when the ruler was shorter. I wonder if a shorter ruler always makes a higher sound.

### What Students Do

1. Students visit one sound station in a small group.
2. They listen first without the iPad.
3. They record a short audio or video clip.
4. They replay the sound and say what they noticed the second time.
5. They record one group wondering using the stem: `We wonder what would happen if...`
6. Groups share one clip and one wondering with the class.

### iPad Enhancement Test

- **Traditional version:** Listen and talk about sounds.
- **Digital function used:** Audio/video capture, replay, oral explanation.
- **Enhancement type:** Replay, student voice, evidence capture.
- **Decision:** Strong. The sound disappears unless captured, and replay helps students notice details they missed live.

**iPad enhancement:** This task is worth using iPads because students can replay sounds several times, notice details they missed live, and preserve oral wonderings as evidence of early scientific thinking.

### Curriculum Fit

- **Science F-2:** Students observe using senses, pose questions, compare observations and communicate ideas using everyday and some scientific vocabulary. Victorian Curriculum Science also explicitly includes slow-motion video and digital tools as appropriate for observing phenomena such as a vibrating ruler.
- **Critical and Creative Thinking F-2:** Students construct and use questions with different stems and describe thinking processes.
- **English F-2:** Students use spoken language, images and sounds to report ideas and events.

### VTLM 2.0 Fit

The teacher first models careful listening, safe recording, the difference between noticing and wondering, and one sentence stem. Students then use the iPad in a short, structured supported application task.

### Framework Fit

| SAMR | EdTech Quintet | PICRAT | TPACK Check |
|---|---|---|---|
| Modification | Mobility + Visualisation + Storytelling | Creative-Amplify | The technology serves the science content because replay makes sound evidence available for repeated observation. |

### Busy Teacher Version

One iPad per group. One sound station. One 10-second recording. One recorded wondering.

## Activity 2: Tiny Worlds Photo Hunt

**Thinking Category:** Notice, Wonder And Explore  
**Student Role:** Students as observers  
**Year Level:** F-2  
**Best Tool:** Camera, Photos Markup, Freeform or Keynote  
**Thinking Tool:** Looking Ten Times Two, simplified for young learners

### Teacher Setup

Choose a small observation area or object set:

- leaves
- bark
- stones
- soil
- classroom materials
- seeds
- shells
- safe evidence of living things

Prepare a simple Freeform board or Keynote slide with:

- `Close-up photo`
- `I notice`
- `I wonder`

### What Students Do

1. Students take 2 or 3 close-up photos of the object or area.
2. They choose the clearest photo.
3. They zoom or crop to show one tiny detail.
4. They add a simple mark, circle or arrow to the detail.
5. They orally record: `I notice...`
6. They orally record: `I wonder...`

### iPad Enhancement Test

- **Traditional version:** Draw the object and talk about what they see.
- **Digital function used:** Close-up photography, zoom/crop, annotation, oral recording.
- **Enhancement type:** Close observation, evidence capture, student voice.
- **Decision:** Keep if students use zoom/crop and oral explanation. Redesign if they only take a decorative photo.

**iPad enhancement:** This task is worth using iPads because zooming and cropping help young students notice tiny details they may miss with normal looking, and audio recording captures their thinking before writing fluency catches up.

### Curriculum Fit

- **Science F-2:** Students make and compare observations about the world around them and record observations using digital tools as appropriate.
- **Humanities F-2:** Students collect, sort and record information from observations, including fieldwork, photographs and local places.
- **Critical and Creative Thinking F-2:** Students use question stems and simple thinking vocabulary.

### VTLM 2.0 Fit

The teacher models observation words such as `smooth`, `rough`, `tiny`, `curved`, `pattern`, `same` and `different`, then students apply those words to captured evidence.

### Framework Fit

| SAMR | EdTech Quintet | PICRAT | TPACK Check |
|---|---|---|---|
| Augmentation to Modification | Mobility + Visualisation | Creative-Amplify | The iPad makes small details visible and supports oral evidence capture for early writers. |

### Busy Teacher Version

Teacher provides 6 close-up photos. Groups choose one, circle a detail and record one `I notice` and one `I wonder`.

## Activity 3: Story Picture Wonder Lens

**Thinking Category:** Notice, Wonder And Explore  
**Student Role:** Students as observers, questioners and storytellers  
**Year Level:** F-2  
**Best Tool:** Camera, Photos Markup, Keynote, Book Creator-style page or Freeform  
**Thinking Tool:** See, Think, Wonder and What Makes You Say That?

### Teacher Setup

Choose a rich picture-book image, illustration, historical photo, classroom object, artefact or story prop. Prepare a simple page with:

- `What do you see?`
- `What do you think might be happening?`
- `What do you wonder?`
- `What makes you say that?`

### What Students Do

1. Students look silently at the image or object first.
2. They use the iPad to zoom in on one detail.
3. They circle or arrow the detail.
4. They record: `I see...`
5. They record: `I think... because...`
6. They record: `I wonder...`
7. Groups listen to another group's response and add one new wondering.

### iPad Enhancement Test

- **Traditional version:** Discuss a picture-book page on the mat.
- **Digital function used:** Zoom/crop, annotation, oral reasoning, peer comparison.
- **Enhancement type:** Close observation, student voice, evidence-linked reasoning.
- **Decision:** Conditional. Strong if students point to the evidence behind their thinking. Weak if they only record guesses.

**iPad enhancement:** This task is worth using iPads when students zoom into visual evidence, annotate the detail behind their thinking and record oral reasoning that can be revisited during reading or inquiry.

### Curriculum Fit

- **English F-2:** Students interpret images and spoken language, share ideas and create simple multimodal responses.
- **Critical and Creative Thinking F-2:** Students use question stems, give examples and describe thinking.
- **Humanities F-2:** When using local or historical images, students ask questions from sources and observations.

### VTLM 2.0 Fit

The teacher first models how an observation is different from a guess, using `What makes you say that?` Students then practise with a visible evidence marker and a short oral response.

### Framework Fit

| SAMR | EdTech Quintet | PICRAT | TPACK Check |
|---|---|---|---|
| Augmentation to Modification | Visualisation + Storytelling + Social | Interactive-Amplify | The iPad supports multimodal evidence-linked talk, not just picture viewing. |

### Busy Teacher Version

Use one projected image. Pairs use one iPad to photograph the image, circle one detail and record one `I wonder` linked to that detail.

## Activity 4: Mystery Material Evidence Lab

**Thinking Category:** Notice, Wonder And Explore  
**Student Role:** Students as observers and early investigators  
**Year Level:** 3-4  
**Best Tool:** Camera, Photos Markup, Keynote or Pages  
**Thinking Tool:** See, Think, Wonder plus property vocabulary

### Teacher Setup

Give each group an object made from mixed materials:

- shoe
- pencil case
- drink bottle
- sports ball
- lunchbox
- backpack
- classroom chair

Prepare a Keynote slide or Pages organiser with:

- `Close-up evidence`
- `Material property words`
- `Test clip`
- `Our wondering`

### What Students Do

1. Students photograph the object close up.
2. They crop the image to focus on one material detail, such as stitching, texture, join, layer or surface.
3. They annotate the photo with property words such as `rough`, `smooth`, `flexible`, `hard`, `soft`, `waterproof`, `shiny`, `absorbent`.
4. They record a 5-second test clip: bending, tapping, stretching, water drop, texture rub or gentle compression.
5. They add one question: `Why is this part made from ___ but this part is made from ___?`
6. Groups compare evidence and identify which questions are worth investigating further.

### iPad Enhancement Test

- **Traditional version:** Draw and label the object.
- **Digital function used:** Close-up photography, video evidence, annotation, replay.
- **Enhancement type:** Close observation, evidence capture, multimodal explanation.
- **Decision:** Strong if students capture actual evidence. Weak if they only make a neat labelled poster.

**iPad enhancement:** This task is worth using iPads because students can capture close-up material evidence and short test clips, then use those images and videos to justify questions about properties and purpose.

### Curriculum Fit

- **Science 3-4:** Students investigate natural and made materials, including fibres, metals, glass and plastics, and consider how properties influence use and re-use.
- **Science Inquiry 3-4:** Students make and record observations using familiar instruments and digital tools as appropriate.
- **Critical and Creative Thinking 3-4:** Students construct open and closed questions for different purposes and use evidence to support reasoning.

### VTLM 2.0 Fit

The teacher first teaches material property vocabulary and models one close-up evidence photo. Students then collect evidence and generate sharper investigation questions.

### Framework Fit

| SAMR | EdTech Quintet | PICRAT | TPACK Check |
|---|---|---|---|
| Modification | Mobility + Visualisation + Storytelling | Creative-Amplify | The technology represents material properties through close-up image and video evidence, supporting science vocabulary and questioning. |

### Busy Teacher Version

One object per group. One close-up photo. Three property labels. One 5-second test clip. One `Why is this made from...?` question.

## Activity 5: School Place Question Trail

**Thinking Category:** Notice, Wonder And Explore  
**Student Role:** Students as observers, researchers and collaborators  
**Year Level:** 3-4  
**Best Tool:** Camera, Voice Memos, Keynote, Freeform or Canva  
**Thinking Tool:** Think, Puzzle, Explore

### Teacher Setup

Choose a small area students can safely investigate:

- school garden
- playground
- oval edge
- recycling area
- water taps
- shaded and unshaded spaces
- local walking route

Prepare a Keynote slide, Freeform board or Canva whiteboard with:

- `Photo evidence`
- `Where was it?`
- `What did we notice?`
- `What do we wonder?`
- `Could this become an inquiry question?`

### What Students Do

1. Groups walk through the chosen place with one iPad.
2. They take 3 or 4 photos of things that make them curious.
3. At the location, they record a short oral question while looking at the evidence.
4. Back in class, they choose the two strongest photo/audio pairs.
5. They add each pair to the organiser.
6. They turn one wondering into a possible inquiry question.
7. The class compares questions and chooses which ones are worth investigating.

### iPad Enhancement Test

- **Traditional version:** Walk, discuss and write questions on paper.
- **Digital function used:** Location-based photo evidence, audio recording in place, class gallery, revisiting evidence.
- **Enhancement type:** Evidence capture, mobility, student voice, collaboration and comparison.
- **Decision:** Keep only when the photo/audio evidence is used later. Optional if students simply walk around and type questions.

**iPad enhancement:** This task is worth using iPads when students capture the actual place that prompted their question, record thinking while the evidence is in front of them, and return to the image later to decide whether the question is researchable.

### Curriculum Fit

- **Humanities F-4:** Students ask geographical questions, collect and record information from observations, fieldwork, photographs and local places.
- **Science 3-4:** Students pose questions to identify patterns and relationships, make predictions based on observations and record information.
- **Personal and Social Capability:** Students practise group roles, turn-taking, active listening and shared decision-making.

### VTLM 2.0 Fit

The teacher models how to turn a noticing into a researchable question. The digital trail becomes the evidence base for a later inquiry decision.

### Framework Fit

| SAMR | EdTech Quintet | PICRAT | TPACK Check |
|---|---|---|---|
| Modification | Mobility + Social + Visualisation | Creative-Amplify | The technology serves place-based inquiry because students capture evidence in the place itself and revisit it later. |

### Busy Teacher Version

Teacher takes 8 photos before the lesson. Groups choose 2 photos, add one noticing, record one question and explain which question is most researchable.

## Activity 6: Living Things Micro-Habitat Walk

**Thinking Category:** Notice, Wonder And Explore  
**Student Role:** Students as observers and early researchers  
**Year Level:** 3-4  
**Best Tool:** Camera, Photos Markup, Keynote or Freeform  
**Thinking Tool:** Looking Ten Times Two and See, Think, Wonder

### Teacher Setup

Choose a safe micro-habitat:

- garden bed
- leaf litter area
- tree base
- compost area
- pond edge, if safe
- shaded and sunny patches

Prepare a group organiser with:

- `Living`
- `Once-living`
- `Non-living`
- `Evidence photo`
- `What might this affect?`
- `Our question`

### What Students Do

1. Students observe without the iPad first.
2. They take 4 or 5 photos of evidence in the micro-habitat.
3. They sort photos into living, once-living and non-living.
4. They annotate one photo with a detail they did not notice at first.
5. They record a group wondering about a relationship, such as `Why are there more insects under the damp leaves?`
6. Groups compare one question and identify what evidence they would need next.

### iPad Enhancement Test

- **Traditional version:** Outdoor observation with clipboard notes.
- **Digital function used:** Photo evidence, zoom/crop, annotation, sorting and revisiting.
- **Enhancement type:** Evidence capture, close observation, collaboration and comparison.
- **Decision:** Strong when students use images to notice details and develop relationship questions. Use clipboards if photos will not be revisited.

**iPad enhancement:** This task is worth using iPads because students can capture fragile, temporary or small evidence from a living place and use it later to generate better habitat questions.

### Curriculum Fit

- **Science 3-4:** Students classify living, once-living and non-living things, investigate organisms in habitats and pose questions from observations.
- **Humanities F-4:** Students observe local places and gather evidence from fieldwork.
- **Critical and Creative Thinking 3-4:** Students use questions to open inquiry and improve thinking.

### VTLM 2.0 Fit

The teacher explicitly models respectful fieldwork, safety and classification vocabulary before students use the iPad to gather evidence.

### Framework Fit

| SAMR | EdTech Quintet | PICRAT | TPACK Check |
|---|---|---|---|
| Modification | Mobility + Visualisation + Social | Creative-Amplify | The technology supports science inquiry by preserving local evidence for later comparison and questioning. |

### Busy Teacher Version

Use teacher-provided photos from the school grounds. Groups sort 6 photos, annotate one detail and record one habitat question.

## Activity 7: Slow Change / Fast Change Investigation Launch

**Thinking Category:** Notice, Wonder And Explore  
**Student Role:** Students as observers and researchers  
**Year Level:** 5-6  
**Best Tool:** Camera time-lapse, slow motion, Keynote or Clips  
**Thinking Tool:** Think, Puzzle, Explore and testable question frames

### Teacher Setup

Choose a phenomenon where the iPad changes what students can observe:

- shadow movement
- seed growth
- evaporation
- erosion tray
- bouncing ball
- vibrating ruler
- light reflection
- dissolving
- mould growth, if handled safely and ethically

Prepare a simple organiser with:

- `Recorded evidence`
- `What we noticed after replay`
- `What changed?`
- `What relationship might be worth testing?`
- `Our researchable question`

### What Students Do

1. Students observe the phenomenon live first.
2. They record it using time-lapse or slow motion.
3. They replay the recording and identify what they noticed the second time.
4. They annotate a screenshot or still frame.
5. They create three questions:
   - one observation question
   - one relationship question
   - one investigation question
6. They choose the strongest investigation question and explain why it could be tested.

Example:

> How does the angle of the light change the length of the shadow?

### iPad Enhancement Test

- **Traditional version:** Observe once and write questions.
- **Digital function used:** Time-lapse or slow motion, replay, annotation, evidence-linked questioning.
- **Enhancement type:** Change over time, replay, researchable questioning.
- **Decision:** Very strong because the iPad changes what students can observe.

**iPad enhancement:** This task is worth using iPads because students can see change over time or movement in detail, then base their questions on recorded evidence rather than memory.

### Curriculum Fit

- **Science 5-6:** Students investigate observable phenomena, analyse patterns and relationships, make reasoned predictions and use equipment to generate and record data with reasonable precision.
- **Critical and Creative Thinking 5-6:** Students construct questions to focus or expand thinking and set aside preconceptions by testing them through evidence.
- **English 5-6:** Students create spoken, written or multimodal explanations using evidence and topic-specific vocabulary.

### VTLM 2.0 Fit

The teacher explicitly teaches the phenomenon vocabulary and models the difference between an observation question and a testable relationship question. Students then apply that distinction to replayable evidence.

### Framework Fit

| SAMR | EdTech Quintet | PICRAT | TPACK Check |
|---|---|---|---|
| Redefinition | Mobility + Visualisation + Feedback loops | Creative-Transform | The iPad transforms the observation by making time, speed and change available for repeated analysis. |

### Busy Teacher Version

Teacher records one time-lapse or slow-motion clip. Groups replay it twice, annotate one screenshot and write one testable question.

## Activity 8: Source Lens Provocation Gallery

**Thinking Category:** Notice, Wonder And Explore  
**Student Role:** Students as researchers and questioners  
**Year Level:** 5-6  
**Best Tool:** Keynote, Canva, Freeform or Pages  
**Thinking Tool:** See, Think, Wonder and What Makes You Say That?

### Teacher Setup

Prepare a small source gallery connected to the unit:

- historical photos
- maps
- graphs
- newspaper headlines
- invention images
- before and after images
- short quotes
- artefact photos
- screenshots from reliable sources

Use a Keynote deck, Canva whiteboard, Freeform board or Pages template with:

- `Source`
- `Cropped evidence`
- `What we notice`
- `What this makes us wonder`
- `Evidence link`
- `How our question changed`

### What Students Do

1. Students inspect 4 to 6 sources in pairs or groups.
2. They choose one source that creates genuine curiosity.
3. They zoom in or crop a specific piece of evidence.
4. They record a 20-second explanation: `This detail made us wonder because...`
5. They write one closed question, one open question and one researchable question.
6. They revise the researchable question after viewing another group's evidence.
7. They save both versions so the teacher can see how the question improved.

### iPad Enhancement Test

- **Traditional version:** Sort questions on sticky notes or poster paper.
- **Digital function used:** Zoom/crop, source evidence, audio reasoning, revision, class gallery.
- **Enhancement type:** Evidence-linked questioning, revision history, collaboration and comparison.
- **Decision:** Conditional. Strong only if questions remain linked to evidence, audio reasoning and revision. Weak if students are just typing questions into boxes.

**iPad enhancement:** This task is worth using iPads only when students use the source itself as evidence: zooming into a detail, recording why it matters, revising their question after comparison and preserving the before/after question for assessment.

### Curriculum Fit

- **Humanities 5-6:** Students develop questions to investigate historical, civic, geographical or contemporary issues and use sources as evidence.
- **Critical and Creative Thinking 5-6:** Students construct questions to focus or expand thinking and evaluate the strength of evidence.
- **English 5-6:** Students interpret multimodal texts and explain how still images, moving images and sound shape meaning or point of view.

### VTLM 2.0 Fit

The teacher explicitly teaches closed, open and researchable questions, then models how evidence can change or improve a question.

### Framework Fit

| SAMR | EdTech Quintet | PICRAT | TPACK Check |
|---|---|---|---|
| Modification, possibly Redefinition if revisited across the unit | Visualisation + Social + Feedback loops | Creative-Amplify | The technology is justified only when source details, oral reasoning and question revision are retained as evidence. |

### Busy Teacher Version

Use one Keynote slide with 4 sources. Each group crops one detail, records one 20-second explanation and improves one researchable question.

## Activity 9: Community Issue Evidence Walk

**Thinking Category:** Notice, Wonder And Explore  
**Student Role:** Students as observers, researchers, collaborators and emerging citizens  
**Year Level:** 5-6  
**Best Tool:** Camera, Voice Memos, Keynote, Freeform, Canva or Pages  
**Thinking Tool:** Think, Puzzle, Explore and The Three Whys

### Teacher Setup

Choose a local issue students can observe safely and respectfully:

- waste and recycling
- shade and heat in the yard
- traffic or movement around the school
- water use
- accessibility
- playground fairness
- biodiversity
- signage and communication
- use of shared spaces

Prepare a digital organiser with:

- `Evidence from our place`
- `What we notice`
- `Who might this affect?`
- `Why might this matter to us?`
- `Why might this matter to our community?`
- `What question should we investigate next?`

### What Students Do

1. Groups collect 3 evidence photos from the chosen place.
2. They record one audio note at the location: `This matters because...`
3. They choose one strongest piece of evidence.
4. They use The Three Whys to ask why it matters to themselves, the school and the wider community.
5. They create one investigable question for a later inquiry or action project.
6. The class sorts questions into `needs more evidence`, `could investigate`, and `could lead to action`.

### iPad Enhancement Test

- **Traditional version:** Walk around and discuss school issues.
- **Digital function used:** Place-based evidence capture, audio, class gallery, sorting and revisiting.
- **Enhancement type:** Evidence capture, student voice, mobility, audience and civic agency.
- **Decision:** Strong if evidence is used to shape later inquiry or action. Low-value if it becomes a photo scavenger hunt.

**iPad enhancement:** This task is worth using iPads because students capture local evidence that can be revisited, compared and used to generate questions about issues that affect their community.

### Curriculum Fit

- **Critical and Creative Thinking 5-6:** Students construct questions to focus thinking, consider evidence and test preconceptions.
- **Personal and Social Capability:** Students practise collaboration, contribution, listening and shared decision-making.

### VTLM 2.0 Fit

The teacher first teaches respectful observation, privacy boundaries and how to frame issues without blaming individuals. Students then gather evidence in a structured way and use it to form better questions.

## Quality Tests For Teachers

### The Why Not Paper Test

Before using iPads, ask:

| Question | Keep iPads If... | Use Paper Instead If... |
|---|---|---|
| Is there evidence to capture? | Students photograph, record, annotate or revisit real evidence. | Students are only listing ideas or copying notes. |
| Does replay matter? | Sound, movement, process or change needs to be heard or seen again. | Students can observe it well enough live. |
| Does magnification matter? | Zoom, crop or close-up reveals meaningful detail. | A drawing, hand lens or discussion would do the same job. |
| Does oral thinking matter? | Audio captures thinking before writing fluency catches up. | Students can record their thinking clearly on paper. |
| Will students revisit this? | Early questions or evidence will be used later in the unit. | The digital artefact will be forgotten after the lesson. |
| Does collaboration add value? | Groups compare evidence, challenge questions and improve thinking. | One student uses the iPad while others watch. |

### The Evidence Test

Every Notice, Wonder And Explore task should leave the teacher with evidence of at least one of these:

- what students observed
- what students assumed
- what students wondered
- what evidence prompted the question
- how the question improved
- what students noticed after replay, zoom, comparison or peer discussion

### The Collaboration Test

Shared iPad work should include roles. For example:

- **Camera operator:** captures evidence, not random photos
- **Evidence spotter:** decides what detail matters
- **Question builder:** turns noticings into questions
- **Voice recorder:** captures the group's thinking
- **Checker:** asks, `What makes us say that?`

For F-2, combine roles and rotate quickly. For 5-6, make each student's contribution visible.

### The Teacher Workload Test

The task should still be practical if:

- there is one iPad per group
- the teacher has less than 10 minutes setup time
- the class only has 20 minutes for the digital part
- the teacher uses a simple template
- students create one strong piece of evidence rather than many weak artefacts

## VTLM 2.0 Fit

This category should not replace explicit teaching. It works best when teachers first model:

- the content vocabulary students need
- what careful observation looks and sounds like

- how to use the chosen digital tool

Preferred wording for teachers:

## SAMR, EdTech Quintet, PICRAT And TPACK Checks

### SAMR

Most strong tasks in this category sit at Augmentation or Modification. A task reaches Redefinition only when the iPad makes previously difficult observation possible, such as time-lapse, slow motion, repeated replay, class-wide evidence galleries or question revision across a unit.

### EdTech Quintet

This category mainly activates:

- **Mobility:** students collect evidence in places, not just at desks
- **Visualisation:** students zoom, crop, annotate and compare details
- **Storytelling:** students record oral observations and wonderings
- **Social:** groups compare evidence and improve questions
- **Feedback loops:** students revisit and refine questions after comparison

### PICRAT

Aim for:

- students as **interactive** when they replay, annotate, sort and compare evidence
- students as **creative** when they produce an evidence-linked artefact
- technology that **amplifies** learning by making thinking visible
- technology that **transforms** learning only when it changes what students can observe, test, revisit or share

Avoid:

- passive viewing
- replacing paper with a digital worksheet
- creative-looking artefacts with weak thinking

### TPACK

The teacher decision is content-specific:

> Does this iPad use help students learn this concept, in this subject, at this stage of learning?

Strong TPACK in this category means teachers can say:

- what the iPad makes visible
- what the iPad might distract from
- what content vocabulary students need first
- when to stop using the iPad and return to talk, paper, modelling or hands-on materials

## Principal-Friendly Rationale

This framework supports a balanced digital learning approach because it does not promote blanket device use. It gives teachers a practical decision rule for purposeful use.

The Notice, Wonder And Explore category helps teachers use iPads when they genuinely improve early inquiry:

- students gather evidence from the real world
- student questions are connected to evidence, not random curiosity
- oral thinking is captured for assessment
- collaboration is structured
- teachers can see what students notice, misunderstand and want to investigate
- digital artefacts are used later, not forgotten

This is consistent with the Victorian direction toward intentional digital learning, explicit teaching, safe and responsible technology use, and reduced passive screen time.

In leadership language:

> Meaningful iPad use is not measured by minutes on device. It is measured by whether the device helps students observe more carefully, think more deeply, collaborate more meaningfully and produce stronger evidence of learning.

## Meeting-Friendly Summary

Notice, Wonder And Explore is the category for starting learning with evidence and curiosity.

The iPad is worthwhile when it helps students capture what would otherwise disappear, such as sound, movement, change, local evidence, small details or oral thinking. It is not worthwhile when it only digitises a brainstorm.

The principal message:

> This framework helps teachers make disciplined decisions about iPad use. We use iPads when they improve observation, questioning, evidence capture, collaboration and assessment. When paper or hands-on materials are better, we use those instead.
