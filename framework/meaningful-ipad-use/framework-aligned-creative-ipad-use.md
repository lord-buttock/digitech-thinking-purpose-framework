# Framework-Aligned Creative iPad Use Across Primary

This document supports compliance with the 2027 Victorian Digital Learning policy by reducing passive, individualised device use and prioritising purposeful, collaborative, creative, curriculum-aligned iPad tasks.

The aim is not "more iPad". The aim is better iPad.

Students should move from passive viewers to active creators: making, explaining, designing, documenting, questioning, composing, publishing, coding, reflecting, critiquing and collaborating.

## Design Position

Meaningful iPad use earns its place when the device helps students:

- make thinking visible
- capture evidence from the world
- explain reasoning
- create an artefact for an audience
- collaborate around a shared purpose
- design, test and refine a solution
- represent data, systems or abstract ideas
- reflect on learning and growth

The device should not be the lesson. It should be the tool that helps students do something cognitively, creatively or collaboratively valuable.

## Frameworks Used

### SAMR

SAMR helps ask whether technology is replacing, improving, significantly redesigning or transforming a task.

- **Substitution:** same task, digital tool.
- **Augmentation:** same task with functional improvement.
- **Modification:** task is significantly redesigned.
- **Redefinition:** task was previously difficult or impossible without technology.

For this policy context, the goal is not to force every task to Redefinition. The goal is to reduce low-value substitution and increase purposeful augmentation, modification and occasional redefinition.

### EdTech Quintet

Puentedura's Quintet helps identify what the technology is doing pedagogically:

- **Social:** communication, collaboration, sharing.
- **Mobility:** learning beyond one place, using portability, camera, audio, accessibility and field capture.
- **Visualisation:** making ideas, data and abstract concepts visible.
- **Storytelling:** communicating learning through narrative, media, voice, sequence and audience.
- **Gaming/feedback loops:** testing, iteration, challenge, feedback and improvement.

### PICRAT

PICRAT is useful because it asks two simple questions:

- What is the student doing? Passive, Interactive or Creative?
- What is technology doing to the task? Replacing, Amplifying or Transforming?

For our school purpose, the priority is to move from **Passive-Replace** toward **Creative-Amplify** and **Creative-Transform**.

### TPACK

TPACK keeps the work grounded:

> Does this technology help teach this content, through this pedagogy, for these learners?

This prevents "cool tech" from replacing curriculum intent. A good iPad task should be content-specific, age-appropriate and pedagogically justified.

## Victorian Curriculum Alignment

This document draws on the Victorian Curriculum materials in [[Education/Curriculum/index]] and the official Digital Technologies curriculum.

Useful curriculum anchors include:

- **Digital Technologies F-2:** students are introduced to digital systems and patterns in data, organise and present data creatively, follow sequences of steps, and create digital solutions through guided play and integrated learning.
- **Digital Technologies 3-4:** students explore digital systems and peripheral devices, collect/manipulate/interpret data, record simple solutions through text and diagrams, and create simple digital solutions using design criteria.
- **Digital Technologies 5-6:** students acquire, validate, interpret, track and manage data, develop abstractions, design algorithms, and manage the creation and communication of digital projects collaboratively using validated data and agreed protocols.
- **English:** students engage with print, digital, visual, multimodal and interactive texts; create spoken, written and multimodal texts for purpose and audience.
- **Science:** students pose questions, conduct investigations, record observations using digital tools where appropriate, process data, and communicate findings.
- **Mathematics:** students collect, represent, interpret and communicate data; use digital tools where appropriate for modelling, representation and investigation.
- **Critical and Creative Thinking:** students generate and evaluate ideas, use criteria to compare proposed solutions, structure arguments, consider evidence and reflect on thinking processes.
- **Personal and Social Capability:** students collaborate, take roles, contribute to group goals and reflect on collaboration.

## Access Model

These examples are designed to work with different device ratios.

| Access model | What it means for planning |
|---|---|
| **Few shared iPads** | Use iPads as a station, evidence-capture tool, group recorder, publishing tool or teacher-led capture device. Most thinking can happen on paper, whiteboards, manipulatives or through talk before the device is used. |
| **Small set, shared between groups** | Groups rotate through creation/capture/editing while others plan, rehearse, build, test, discuss or reflect offline. |
| **Class set / 1:1 booking** | Use rare full-access moments for tasks that genuinely benefit from every student creating, rehearsing, annotating, responding, drafting, coding, reflecting or contributing to a shared digital artefact. |

## When A Class Set Is Worth Booking

A class set should be used for short, high-value bursts where individual access improves the learning. Full access is most defensible when every student needs to create, test, annotate, record, respond or reflect individually before returning to discussion, collaboration or teacher feedback.

| Class set / 1:1 moment | What students do individually | How it returns to collaboration |
|---|---|---|
| Individual capture burst | Each student photographs evidence, examples or observations from a shared environment. | Students pool examples, sort them, identify patterns and build a shared evidence board. |
| Annotation burst | Each student annotates a text, image, graph, model or worked example. | Pairs compare annotations and agree on the strongest evidence or most important idea. |
| Explanation burst | Each student records a short oral explanation or screen recording. | Groups choose one strong explanation, improve it, or combine parts into a group tutorial. |
| Draft and feedback burst | Each student drafts, records or designs a small component. | Peers give criteria-based feedback, then students revise or contribute to a shared product. |
| Coding/testing burst | Each pair or student tests an algorithm, interactive story, quiz or simulation. | Students compare what worked, identify bugs, and co-create debugging advice. |
| Reflection burst | Each student captures what changed in their thinking. | Groups look for common shifts, unresolved questions and next learning steps. |

Avoid using class sets for whole-lesson passive viewing, silent scrolling, routine worksheets or reading that could happen just as well on paper.

## Design Rules For Teachers

- Start with the learning, not the app.
- Use the iPad after students have something worth capturing, creating or explaining.
- Build in talk before touch: plan, rehearse, decide roles, then use the device.
- Prefer group artefacts when devices are limited.
- Use class sets for short, high-value bursts, not full-lesson passive use.
- Make every iPad task produce evidence of learning.
- If the task is only watching, reading or clicking, redesign it.

## Shared Digital Canvas Pattern

Many of the collaborative examples work best when the teacher sets up a simple shared digital canvas before the lesson. This could be a Freeform board, a Keynote slide, a Pages template, or another app the school already uses.

The important point is that the iPad is not just where students "do the work". It is where the group collects evidence, makes relationships visible, records explanations and preserves the final artefact for sharing or assessment.

Simple classroom models:

| Model                           | How It Works                                                                                                                                                                     |
| ------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **One iPad per group**          | The group uses one iPad as the capture and creation tool. Students take photos on that iPad, place them onto the group canvas, add labels/arrows, and record short explanations. |
| **Teacher-created class board** | The teacher prepares one Freeform board or Keynote file with sections or prompts. Groups contribute to a copy, a shared board, or a projected class version.                     |
| **Class set burst**             | If a class set is booked, students each capture one photo, note, question, annotation or voice explanation, then share it back to the group board for curation.                  |
| **Paper-first, iPad-second**    | Students begin with talk, sticky notes, manipulatives or poster paper. The iPad is then used to photograph, annotate, explain, revise and preserve the thinking.                 |

## Paper-To-iPad Enhancement Guide

Many of these tasks can begin on paper. That is a strength, not a problem. The iPad is justified only when it adds evidence, voice, movement, audience, revision, feedback, accessibility, collaboration, data handling or a form of representation that paper cannot easily provide.

Use this section as the quality gate before booking devices.

| Task | Paper Is Enough If... | The iPad Elevates The Task When... | Simple Shared-iPad Move |
|---|---|---|---|
| Make thinking visible | Students only need to brainstorm, sort, draw links or make a poster-sized concept map. | Students add photographed evidence, audio explanations of key links, revision layers showing how thinking changed, or combine several groups' maps into a class gallery. | Build the map on paper first. Photograph it, then add 3 voice notes: strongest connection, most surprising connection, and one question still unresolved. |
| Capture inquiry evidence | Students are recording observations that can be sketched or written easily. | Students need accurate visual evidence, close-up details, before/after comparisons, location-based evidence, or a record that can be revisited for assessment. | One evidence-capturer takes 4 photos for the group: example, non-example, close-up detail, and question/wonder. |
| Record observations over time | Students only need one-off observations. | Change over time matters: growth, weather, shadows, Moon, material changes, decay, movement, repeated trials or patterns. | Use one class iPad at the same time each day. Students add a sentence: "What changed since last time?" |
| Slow-motion science or PE analysis | Students can observe the phenomenon clearly in real time. | The learning depends on seeing what is too fast, subtle or repeatable: bounce, vibration, push/pull, throw, jump, collision or reaction. | Record one test in slow motion. Pause on 2 key frames and label what the evidence shows. |
| Record a maths strategy | Students only need to write a solution. | The teacher needs to hear reasoning, compare strategies, diagnose misconceptions, or support students who explain better orally than in writing. | Each group records one 45-second explanation after solving on whiteboards. The group must name the strategy and justify one step. |
| Create misconception explainers | Students are simply correcting answers. | Students diagnose why an error happens, explain the misconception, create advice for peers, or build a reusable revision resource. | Give groups one common error. They record: "The mistake is..., it happened because..., the fix is..." |
| Oral rehearsal before writing | Students can plan and rehearse effectively through talk with a partner. | Students benefit from hearing themselves, revising spoken ideas, supporting EAL/oral language, or turning oral rehearsal into a writing scaffold. | At a recording station, pairs record their paragraph orally, listen once, then improve the written version. |
| Peer tutorial | Students are just making a display or copying steps. | Students must teach a real audience, demonstrate a process, use voice and visuals together, or create a resource other students can replay. | Groups plan on paper, then record a short tutorial for one process, strategy or concept. |
| Mini documentary | A poster/report would communicate the learning well enough. | Students need to combine evidence, narration, images, interviews, location footage, sequence and audience into a coherent explanation. | Groups collect only 3 media pieces: evidence image, short interview/audio, and final explanation. Keep the edit simple. |
| Podcast panel or interview | Students only need a class discussion. | Listening, questioning, speaking fluency, expert/community voice, or audience communication are central to the learning. | One iPad is the recording booth. Students bring 3 prepared questions and record a 2-minute panel or interview. |
| Character, expert or stakeholder interview | Students can show perspective through role-play or writing. | Students need to rehearse voice, tone, evidence-based perspective-taking, or compare multiple stakeholder viewpoints. | Record the interview after the script is checked for evidence. Each answer must include "because..." |
| Digital museum exhibit | Students are making labels for physical artefacts only. | Students add audio guides, video demonstrations, QR-linked explanations, accessibility support, visitor feedback or a digital archive. | Keep artefacts physical. Use the iPad to record a 30-second curator explanation for each exhibit. |
| Local place trail | Students can draw a map and present in class. | Mobility matters: students capture real locations, create an audio/photo trail, guide visitors, or connect learning to place. | Groups visit one location, take one photo and record one explanation: "This place matters because..." |
| Animate an abstract concept | Students can show the sequence clearly with drawings or manipulatives. | Movement, sequence, transformation, cause/effect or invisible processes need to be made visible: lifecycles, fractions, forces, grammar, food chains, systems. | Build with paper/manipulatives first. Capture 8-12 stop-motion frames and record one explanation of what changed. |
| Model explanation | Students can present their physical model directly. | The model needs labelled close-ups, a preserved explanation, repeated viewing, peer feedback, or comparison between first and improved versions. | Build offline. Record a "model tour" with 3 labelled parts and one limitation of the model. |
| Data story | Students only need a tally, table or graph. | Students need to combine data, visual representation, interpretation, evidence and recommendation for an audience. | Collect data on paper. Use the iPad to photograph the graph and record: "The pattern we noticed is..., so we recommend..." |
| Source/evidence board | Teacher has already provided a small set of printed sources. | Students need to compare digital sources, capture source details, evaluate credibility, annotate evidence, or justify source choices. | Use limited iPads only for source checking. Each group adds "useful because..." and "limitation..." to each source. |
| Design solution pitch | Students can explain their prototype face-to-face. | Audience, persuasion, recorded feedback, iteration history or wider sharing matters. | Build and test physically. Record a pitch: problem, user, solution, test result, next improvement. |
| App or interface prototype | Paper wireframes are enough to show layout and flow. | Students need to simulate interaction, test usability, collect user feedback, or show screen sequence clearly. | Create paper screens first. Photograph them in order and record a user walking through the flow. |
| Coding an interactive story | The task is really a written branching story only. | Students are learning sequence, events, branching, conditionals, user choice or debugging through an interactive digital product. | Storyboard on paper. Use iPads only when the logic is ready to build and test. |
| Coding a simulation or model | Students can model the idea physically or with a diagram. | Students need to test variables, run repeated trials, show systems behaviour, or compare model output with real-world expectations. | Plan the model unplugged. Use the iPad to build/test one variable and record what the model can and cannot show. |
| Robotics debugging journal | Students are simply trying a robot path once. | The learning is in debugging: prediction, test, failure, revision, retest and explanation. | One student records each test. After each attempt, the group adds one sentence: "We changed ___ because ___." |
| Student-created quiz or challenge | Students are writing recall questions only. | Students design diagnostic questions, feedback, misconceptions, peer challenge or retrieval practice that others can use. | Groups write questions on paper, then use the iPad to publish only the best 3 with explanations for wrong answers. |
| Digital campaign | A poster will reach the intended audience and show the learning. | Students need multiple media formats, audience testing, persuasive voice, recorded message, distribution or impact reflection. | Start with a paper message map. Use iPad to create one campaign asset and collect 3 audience responses. |
| Peer critique gallery | Students can walk around and leave sticky-note feedback. | Students need recorded oral feedback, before/after evidence, criteria-linked comments, or a preserved feedback trail for revision. | Photograph each artefact. Peers record one "warm" and one "cool" feedback comment linked to criteria. |
| Learning journey portfolio | The teacher only needs a final product. | Growth, process, drafts, mistakes, feedback and revision are important evidence of learning. | At milestones, one iPad station captures a photo and a 20-second reflection: "What changed? What did we improve?" |
| Class knowledge bank | Students are making one-off posters for the classroom wall. | The class is building a reusable resource with audio, video, examples, vocabulary, misconceptions or future audience value. | Each group contributes one short explanation to a shared bank. Keep entries under 90 seconds. |
| Digital citizenship scenario | Students can discuss safe/responsible choices through role-play. | Students need to rehearse, review, critique, share and refine examples of responsible digital behaviour. | Record a scenario twice: the risky choice and the responsible choice. Discuss what changed. |
| Student voice consultation | A simple class vote is enough. | Students need to collect broader evidence, compare responses, interview stakeholders, represent data and present recommendations. | Small teams collect responses with one iPad, then the group makes a recommendation using evidence. |

## Enhancement Test

Before using iPads, ask:

| Question | If yes... | If no... |
|---|---|---|
| Does the iPad capture evidence students could not easily preserve on paper? | Use it for camera, audio, video, time-lapse, slow motion or close-up detail. | Use paper, whiteboards or manipulatives. |
| Does the iPad make student reasoning more visible? | Use it for voiceover, screen recording, annotation or explanation. | Ask students to explain orally or write on paper. |
| Does the iPad support revision over time? | Use it for before/after captures, portfolios or feedback records. | Use drafts, sticky notes or teacher conference notes. |
| Does the iPad connect to a real audience? | Use it for publishing, gallery walks, audio guides, campaigns or tutorials. | Present live or use a physical display. |
| Does the iPad let students test, simulate or debug? | Use it for coding, robotics, modelling, repeated trials or slow-motion analysis. | Use unplugged modelling first. |
| Does the iPad improve accessibility or participation? | Use it for audio rehearsal, translation support, oral response, visual prompts or multimodal expression. | Keep the task offline. |
| Is the iPad only making the work neater or more colourful? | Use paper instead. | Use paper instead. |

## Passive To Active Redesigns

| Instead Of... | Shift To... | Why This Is Better |
|---|---|---|
| Watching a YouTube video | Students create a 60-second explainer, critique the video, compare it with another source, or make a response diagram. | Students process, evaluate and communicate rather than simply receive. |
| Reading a digital book silently | Students annotate, record fluent reading, perform a scene, create a soundscape, compare settings, or produce a character interview. | Reading becomes expressive, interpretive and collaborative. |
| Completing a digital worksheet | Students photograph evidence, explain reasoning, create a worked example, diagnose an error or make a peer tutorial. | The artefact reveals thinking, not just answers. |
| Searching Google | Students build an evidence board, compare sources, rank usefulness, or justify which source they trust. | Research becomes source evaluation and synthesis. |
| Typing a final copy | Students use digital tools for planning, peer feedback, revision tracking, multimodal publishing or audience-specific design. | The technology supports writing process and audience. |
| Playing a practice game | Students design a quiz/challenge with feedback that targets common misconceptions. | Students become instructional designers, not point collectors. |
| Teacher showing slides | Students create one slide/visual that explains a concept, misconception or example to peers. | Students generate representations instead of viewing them. |

## Simple Planning Template

Use this with teachers when redesigning a lesson.

| Prompt | Teacher Notes |
|---|---|
| What is the learning intention? |  |
| What would students normally do passively? | Watch, read, copy, click, answer, complete. |
| What could students create instead? | Explain, capture, design, model, record, annotate, code, publish, reflect. |
| How many iPads are realistically available? | Few shared / small set / class set. |
| What can happen before the iPad is used? | Talk, plan, rehearse, sketch, build, sort, draft. |
| What does the iPad add? | Evidence, audience, visualisation, collaboration, mobility, feedback, creation. |
| What artefact will show learning? | Photo board, audio, video, map, model, code, portfolio, data story, reflection. |
| How will students collaborate? | Roles, shared goal, individual accountability, peer feedback. |
| What will the teacher look for? | Specific evidence of understanding, reasoning, vocabulary, process or revision. |

## Quick Decision Tool

Before using iPads, ask:

1. Are students using the iPad to consume, respond or create?
2. Does the iPad replace, amplify or transform the task?
3. Is the task social, mobile, visual, story-based or feedback-rich?
4. Does the iPad help teach this content through a stronger pedagogy?
5. What evidence of learning will the teacher see?
6. Can the task still work with limited devices?

If the answer is mostly "consume" and "replace", redesign the task.
