# Investigate, Connect And Explain

> Students use iPads to gather evidence, make relationships visible, explain reasoning and show how ideas, causes, evidence, events, data or perspectives connect.

This category sits after curiosity and first observation. It is where students start making meaning.

The important shift is:

> The iPad is not for arranging ideas neatly.  
> The iPad is for connecting ideas to evidence, voice, movement, data, source links, revision, comparison or audience.

## Thinking Category Spec

### Thinking Job

Students move from collecting information to making meaning. They connect evidence, ideas, events, data, causes, effects, processes or perspectives, then explain the relationship.

### Best Used When

- students need to explain how or why something happens
- students need to connect evidence to a claim
- students need to compare findings across groups
- students need to show cause and effect
- students need to explain change over time
- students need to make relationships visible
- students need to turn observations into evidence-based explanation

### Student Roles

- Students as explainers
- Students as researchers
- Students as collaborators
- Students as storytellers

### Strong iPad Artefacts

- explainer videos
- annotated media
- podcasts
- data stories
- interactive timelines
- relationship organisers
- stop-motion explanations
- source-linked evidence boards

### Enhancement Rule

If students are only arranging ideas, use paper.

If students are linking ideas to evidence, recording reasoning, showing change, comparing versions, explaining to an audience, visualising data or using movement/animation to show a process, the iPad can add genuine value.

### Substitution Danger Zone

Avoid:

- digital mind maps with no evidence
- typed brainstorms
- drag-and-drop sorting with no explanation
- food-chain posters with arrows but no reasoning
- timelines with dates and pictures but no causation
- slideshows with no audience or explanation
- digital worksheets that ask students to fill boxes without creating or explaining

### Required Evidence Of Learning

Each activity should produce at least one assessable trace of thinking:

- recorded explanation
- annotated evidence
- source link
- revised version
- before/after comparison
- student-created model
- data interpretation
- peer feedback and revision

## Example: F-2 Photo Explain-It

**Thinking Category:** Investigate, Connect And Explain  
**Student Role:** Students as explainers  
**Year Level:** F-2  
**Best Tool:** Camera, Photos Markup, Clips, Voice Memos

### Teacher Setup

Choose a concrete classroom or inquiry moment where students can explain something visible:

- why one material was better for a purpose
- how a plant changed
- what happened when an object was pushed or pulled
- how a character solved a problem
- what changed in a class construction or experiment

Model one sentence:

> This photo shows ___. We think this happened because ___.

### What Students Do

1. Students take or use one photo of real evidence.
2. They circle or point to the part of the photo that matters.
3. They rehearse one explanation with a partner.
4. They record a short voice explanation: `This shows... because...`
5. The teacher chooses 2-3 examples to compare: `What evidence did each group use?`

### iPad Enhancement Test

- **Traditional version:** Draw and tell, or paste a photo into a workbook.
- **Digital function used:** Photo evidence, annotation, audio explanation, sharing for comparison.
- **Enhancement type:** Evidence capture, student voice, multimodal explanation.
- **Decision:** Keep if the audio explains the evidence. Redesign if it is only a labelled image.

**iPad enhancement:** This task is worth using iPads because the photo preserves the evidence and the audio captures young students' reasoning before writing fluency catches up.

### Curriculum Fit

- **Science F-2:** Students make and compare observations, identify patterns, pose questions and communicate observations and ideas.
- **English F-2:** Students create short spoken and multimodal texts using images and familiar vocabulary.
- **Critical and Creative Thinking F-2:** Students use words that show reasons and conclusions and describe thinking processes.

### VTLM 2.0 Fit

Best used after explicit teacher modelling of the target explanation stem and vocabulary. Students then apply the model with their own evidence.

### Framework Fit

| SAMR | EdTech Quintet | PICRAT |
|---|---|---|
| Augmentation to Modification | Visualisation + Storytelling | Creative-Amplify |

### Busy Teacher Version

Teacher provides 4 photos from a shared class experience. Each pair chooses one photo, circles one detail and records one sentence.

## Example: 3-4 Habitat Relationship Explanation

**Thinking Category:** Investigate, Connect And Explain  
**Student Role:** Students as explainers and collaborators  
**Year Level:** 3-4  
**Best Tool:** Keynote, Freeform, Canva, Camera, audio recording

### Teacher Setup

Create a Keynote, Freeform or Canva organiser with labelled sections:

- `Producers`
- `Consumers`
- `Decomposers`
- `Connections`
- `What would happen if...?`

Provide 6-8 organism images linked to a familiar habitat, or let students photograph/draw/import images after explicit teaching.

### What Students Do

1. Students place organisms into the organiser.
2. They draw arrows showing feeding or dependency relationships.
3. For each arrow, they add a short label: `eats`, `depends on`, `breaks down`, `provides shelter`.
4. Each student records one 10-20 second explanation of one relationship.
5. The group adds one `What would happen if...?` consequence note.
6. Groups revise one connection after viewing another group's organiser.

### iPad Enhancement Test

- **Traditional version:** Food-chain poster with pictures and arrows.
- **Digital function used:** Movable images, annotation, audio explanation, revision, class gallery.
- **Enhancement type:** Multimodal explanation, collaboration and comparison, revision history.
- **Decision:** Conditional. Strong if students justify relationships with recorded reasoning and revise after feedback; optional if it is only a neat digital poster.

**iPad enhancement:** This task is worth using iPads because the arrows become explainable and revisable evidence of reasoning, not just lines on a poster. Students can record why each relationship matters and update their model after comparison.

### Curriculum Fit

- **Science 3-4:** Students explore living things, habitats, needs, observable features and relationships.
- **Science Inquiry 3-4:** Students organise information, identify patterns and relationships and communicate findings.
- **Critical and Creative Thinking 3-4:** Students use evidence to support reasoning and communicate conclusions with reasons.

### VTLM 2.0 Fit

Best used after explicit teaching of habitat vocabulary and modelled examples of producer, consumer, decomposer and dependency relationships.

### Framework Fit

| SAMR | EdTech Quintet | PICRAT |
|---|---|---|
| Modification | Visualisation + Social + Storytelling | Creative-Amplify |

### Busy Teacher Version

One iPad per group. Six teacher-provided organism images. Four arrows. One recorded explanation per group.

## Example: 5-6 Interactive Cause-And-Effect Timeline

**Thinking Category:** Investigate, Connect And Explain  
**Student Role:** Students as researchers, explainers and storytellers  
**Year Level:** 5-6  
**Best Tool:** Keynote, Canva, Pages, Book Creator-style workflow

### Teacher Setup

Choose a topic where sequence alone is not enough:

- innovations that changed society
- environmental change in a place
- development of a technology
- events in a historical inquiry
- stages in a scientific process
- changes in community needs over time

Prepare a simple timeline template with:

- `Event / change`
- `Evidence`
- `What caused it?`
- `What changed because of it?`
- `Source or image`
- `Voice explanation`

### What Students Do

1. Students select 4-5 key events or changes.
2. For each point, they add an image or source.
3. They add one cause and one effect.
4. They record a short explanation for the most important connection.
5. They add links or buttons so viewers can tap to explore the evidence.
6. They revise one timeline point after peer feedback: `Does this show what changed and why?`

### iPad Enhancement Test

- **Traditional version:** Paper timeline with dates and drawings.
- **Digital function used:** Images, source links, voice narration, interactivity, revision and audience.
- **Enhancement type:** Interactivity, source-linked explanation, storytelling, revision.
- **Decision:** Strong if students explain causation and evidence. Weak if they only list dates with pictures.

**iPad enhancement:** This task is worth using iPads because students can turn a static timeline into an interactive explanation where each point includes evidence, narration and cause-effect reasoning for an audience.

### Curriculum Fit

- **Humanities 5-6:** Students develop questions, use sources as evidence and explain change, continuity, causes and impacts.
- **English 5-6:** Students create structured spoken, written and multimodal texts for purpose and audience.
- **Critical and Creative Thinking 5-6:** Students evaluate evidence, identify reasoning and use questions to focus or expand thinking.

### VTLM 2.0 Fit

Best used after explicit teaching of cause and effect, source use and timeline structure. The iPad artefact is the supported application where students explain relationships for an audience.

### Framework Fit

| SAMR | EdTech Quintet | PICRAT |
|---|---|---|
| Modification to Redefinition | Storytelling + Visualisation + Social | Creative-Transform |

### Busy Teacher Version

Use 4 teacher-provided events. Groups add one image, one cause, one effect and one 30-second voice explanation.

## Meeting-Friendly Summary

**Investigate, Connect And Explain** is the category for moving from information to meaning.

Students are not using iPads to make prettier organisers. They are using iPads to:

- connect claims to evidence
- explain relationships orally and visually
- show cause and effect
- interpret data
- revise models after feedback
- create explanations for an audience

The most teacher-friendly starting point is:

> One iPad per group. One piece of evidence. One relationship. One recorded explanation.
