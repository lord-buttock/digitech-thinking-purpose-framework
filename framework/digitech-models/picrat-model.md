## Summary

The primary academic source for the PICRAT model — a student-focused technology integration framework. PICRAT uses two questions to evaluate any classroom technology activity:

1. **What are students doing with the technology?** → **PIC:** Passive / Interactive / Creative
2. **How does technology impact the teacher's pedagogy?** → **RAT:** Replacement / Amplification / Transformation

The 3×3 matrix produces 9 cells. The goal is to move toward the top-right (Creative-Transformation). The article also critically evaluates SAMR, TPACK, TIM, TIP, TAM, LoTi, and RAT — finding each has weaknesses PICRAT addresses.

## Key Points

- **PIC:** Passive = students consume; Interactive = students engage with content/peers; Creative = students construct artefacts (highest Bloom's level)
- **RAT:** Replacement = technology replaces prior practice with no gain; Amplification = incremental improvement; Transformation = technology enables pedagogy that could not exist without it
- Most teachers start at Passive-Replacement — push toward Creative-Amplify/Transform
- PICRAT is evaluated at the **activity level** — the same teacher uses multiple cells across a unit
- **SAMR critique:** boundary between Substitution/Augmentation is unclear; student activities not explicit
- **TPACK critique:** too comprehensive for everyday use; lacks practical "engineering power"
- **PICRAT strength:** clear (just 2 questions, 9 cells), student-centred, treats technology as a means not an end

## PLC / Coaching Use

Ask teachers: *"Where does this activity sit on the PICRAT matrix? What would Creative-Amplify look like here?"*

Use alongside SAMR — PICRAT is more student-focused and simpler for activity-level reflection; SAMR is useful for whole-task or whole-unit planning conversations.
